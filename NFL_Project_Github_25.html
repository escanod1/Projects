Executive Summary

Our project looked into how weather affects NFL performance through different variables such as wind speed, temperature, stadium type, and home field advantage. We used NFL data from the 2013 season. We applied statistical methods like maximum likelihood estimates, regression models, and visualizations to identify potential trends, and determine the effect of weather on game outcomes.

Key Findings:

  • Temperature and wind speed had minimal effect on scoring.

  • Home teams won 61.5% of games, suggesting some advantage.

  • Games in domes saw slightly higher scoring, but the difference was not statistically significant.

  • Environmental factors explained very little of the variation in total points.

    Our analysis suggests that while teams may adjust strategies based on weather, these factors do not strongly determine game score. Home field advantage is small, and not statistically sound enough to conclude any significant difference.

Introduction

American football is the most popular sport in the United States. Generating over $23 billion in the last fiscal year, the sport is very popular, drawing in fans from all around the globe. Fans take games very seriously, and multiple factors contribute to winning a game. Some industries are very interested in predicting the outcomes of games, such as the sports betting industry, which generates billions of dollars each year. Without a doubt, coaching, training, team culture, and injuries contribute to the outcome of games. Other factors that can contribute to the outcomes of games are whether or not it is a home game (teams playing at home may have a slight advantage due to familiarity and being backed by more fans), simple luck, and weather. It is not always clear how the weather affects the outcome of games. As the sport of football and many other sports grow more data-driven, understanding the impact of external factors has become even more important. One of the least predictable and least understood variables is weather. Unlike fixed elements like team rosters or stadium location, weather conditions can change quickly and vary each game. Rain, snow, wind, or extreme temperatures can alter game strategy, player performance, and even increase risk of injury. For this project, my team and I will seek to determine how significant the weather is in the outcome of NFL games.

Home teams won approximately 61.49 percent of the games, while away teams won approximately 37.84 percent of the games during the 2013 season. This suggests that playing at home provides a real advantage, likely due to factors such as crowd support, reduced travel, and familiarity with the stadium. However, while helpful, home field advantage is not an overwhelming guarantee of winning.

To determine whether home teams won significantly more often than expected by chance, we conducted a chi-squared goodness-of-fit test. Out of all games in the 2013 season, home teams won approximately 61.5% of the time. Under the null hypothesis of no home field advantage, we would expect a 50/50 split between home and away wins. The chi-squared test yielded a test statistic of Ï‡Â² = 10.0 with a p-value of 0.001, indicating that the observed distribution of wins is significantly different from what would be expected by chance. There is statistically significant evidence (at the 0.05 level) to suggest that home teams had a real advantage in the 2013 NFL season.

Why the Home Field Advantage Conclusion Might Be Misleading

Although the home team won about 62 percent of the time, this statistic does not prove that location alone caused the wins. Stronger teams often have better regular season records and get to host more home playoff games, which could inflate the win percentage. Other variables such as overall team strength, injuries, and coaching decisions play important roles and may be more influential than simply playing at home.

The histograms of home team scores and away team scores show that home teams tended to score slightly more points than visiting teams during the 2013 season. While the overall shapes of the distributions were similar, the slight rightward shift of the home score distribution supports the idea that home field advantage includes a small offensive benefit.

Our findings indicate that weather variables like wind speed, and temperature have no significant effects on NFL scoring. However when it comes to field goal percentage, we did find statistically significant evidence suggesting that wind speed does affect field goal percentage. The lack of strong statistical significance in several models reinforces the idea that scoring outcomes are driven more by team skill, strategy, and in-game decisions than by environmental conditions alone.

Conclusion and Future Work

This study shows that in the NFL, environmental factors like wind and temperature play a limited role in determining game outcomes or points scored. Home field advantage appears to offer a small benefit, but deeper investigation is needed to separate team quality from location effects.

Future research could include:
